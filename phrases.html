Phrases

A Phrase phrase is a group of words that stand together as a single grammatical unit, typically as part of a clause or a sentence. A phrase does not contain a subject and verb and, consequently, cannot convey a complete thought. A phrase contrasts with a clause. A clause does contain a subject and verb, and it can convey a complete idea.

Types of Phrases

 Noun phrase - is any noun or pronoun along with its modifiers:

Examples:

a) Yesterday’s newspaper

b) The School children

c) An old and rusted slinky

 Verb phrase - is any number of verbs working together:

Examples:

a) May have written

b) Had been sleeping

c) Will contact

 Prepositional phrase - always starts with a preposition and ends with a noun or pronoun (and its modifiers) that is called the object of the preposition:

Examples:

a) Object of the preposition: the wheat field

b) Preposition: through

c) Through the wheat field

 Verbal phrases - There are three types of verbal phrases: participial phrases, gerund phrases, and infinitive phrases. Each is explained below.

• Participial phrase phrase - Participial phrases start with either a present or past participle.

Examples for Phrases with present participants:

a) Lounging by the pool

b) Chasing a butterfly

c) Watching silently


Examples for Phrases with past participants:

a) Lounging by the pool

b) Chasing a butterfly

c) Watching silently


• Gerund phrase - is a present participle (and its modifiers) that acts like a noun. It can take on a variety of jobs in the sentence.

Examples:

a) Practicing helped a lot. (subject)

b) I love reading. (direct object)


• Infinitive phrase - is the infinitive and its modifiers:

Examples:

a) To sing

b) To walk all that way

c) To mix peanut butter and jam

 Appositive phrase - is a phrase that renames an earlier noun or pronoun:

Examples:

a) My best friend, Nick Palacio, loves scuba diving.

b) We watched Sirius, the brightest star in the sky.

 Absolute phrase - are not closely connected to the rest of the sentence; they don’t describe a specific word, but modify the whole sentence. They add extra information and are usually separated by commas (or dashes).

Examples:

a) The tide coming in, most beachgoers were packing up.

Absolute phrase:The tide coming in.

b) The semester finished, Karen sold all her textbooks.

Absolute phrase:The semester finished.
